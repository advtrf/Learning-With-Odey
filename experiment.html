Experiment 1
How Does the Concentration of Vitamin C Affect Apple Browning?
🌿 SDG 3 — Good Health & Well-Being: Food Preservation & Safety
🎯 Objective
To investigate the effect of different concentrations of citric acid (Vitamin C) as a natural food preservative on the browning rate of apple slices, and to relate findings to safe food preservation practices supporting SDG 3.
🌍 SDG 3 Connection
3
Good Health & Well‑Being

Why does this connect to SDG 3?

Food spoilage causes millions of cases of foodborne illness annually. Understanding safe, natural preservatives like Vitamin C (citric acid) helps reduce reliance on dangerous banned preservatives (formalin, borax), directly supporting SDG 3 Target 3.9: "Reduce illnesses from hazardous chemicals."

📖 Background

Apple browning is caused by enzymatic oxidation — when apple flesh is exposed to air, an enzyme called polyphenol oxidase (PPO) reacts with oxygen, producing brown pigments (melanins). Citric acid (Vitamin C / ascorbic acid) acts as an antioxidant that inhibits this reaction, slowing browning significantly.

Your teacher has prepared 4 apple samples (A, B, C, D), each treated with a different concentration of citric acid and stored for 2 weeks. The concentrations are unknown to you — your job is to figure them out!

Experiment 2
Are the Colors in Our Food Natural or Artificial? The Wool Thread Test
🌿 SDG 3 — Good Health & Well-Being: Food Safety Awareness
🎯 Objective
To identify whether food samples contain artificial (synthetic) or natural colorants using the wool thread absorption method, and to relate findings to food safety and SDG 3.
🌍 SDG 3 Connection
3
Good Health & Well‑Being

Why does this connect to SDG 3?

Banned colorants like Rhodamine B have been linked to carcinogenicity and allergic reactions. By learning to detect artificial colorants, we build food safety literacy that directly supports SDG 3 Target 3.9: "Reduce deaths and illnesses from hazardous chemicals and pollution."

⚠️ Safety & Materials
⚠️
Safety: Do not consume samples after testing. Ask adult supervision when using the stove. Wash hands before and after.
🧶White wool thread (10 cm pieces)
🥤3–5 food/drink samples
🧴White vinegar (5% acetic acid)
🔥Stove or portable heater
🫙Small heat-safe cups
🥄Tongs or spoon
⏱️Timer (10 minutes)
🧼Soap and water for rinsing
🔍 Inquiry Procedure
💡 Answer each guiding question — your answers ARE the procedure. Observe, think, and investigate!
1
Look at your food samples before starting. Which samples do you think contain artificial colorants, and which contain natural ones? What visual clues are you using to make this prediction?
✍️ Initial Prediction
2
You will add vinegar to each sample before boiling. Why do you think the vinegar is needed? What role does acidity play in this test? Make a prediction before you proceed.
✍️ My Reasoning
3
Now carry out the test: pour ~100 mL of each sample into a cup, add 1 tsp vinegar, submerge one wool thread, and boil for 10 minutes. After boiling, remove and rinse the thread with soap and water for 30 seconds. What do you observe about each thread immediately after boiling? After rinsing?
✍️ My Observations (before and after rinsing)
4
Based on your results, which samples do you now classify as artificial vs natural colorant? How confident are you in this classification? Does this match your original prediction?
✍️ My Classification & Confidence

Experiment 3
Does Caffeine Change Your Heart Rate? An Inquiry into Addictive Substances
🌿 SDG 3 — Good Health & Well-Being: Addictive Substance Awareness
🎯 Objective
To investigate the short-term physiological effect of caffeine on heart rate, and to relate findings to SDG 3 through awareness of how addictive substances affect human health.
🌍 SDG 3 Connection
3
Good Health & Well‑Being

Why does this connect to SDG 3?

Caffeine is the world's most consumed psychoactive substance. While legal, excess caffeine causes dependence, anxiety, and cardiovascular effects. This experiment supports SDG 3 Target 3.5: "Strengthen prevention and treatment of substance abuse" — even mild substances deserve awareness.

⚠️ Safety & Materials
⚠️
Important: Do not participate if you have a heart condition or caffeine sensitivity. Students under 13 should observe a consenting adult volunteer. Use only 1 standard serving (150 mL coffee or tea).
☕1 cup coffee or tea (~150 mL)
⏱️Timer/stopwatch
🫀Wrist pulse (radial artery)
💺Chair — stay seated throughout
👥1–3 volunteer subjects
📋This worksheet
🔍 Inquiry Procedure
💡 Answer each guiding question to guide your investigation. Your thinking IS the experiment!
1
Before consuming any caffeine — what do you already know about how caffeine affects the body? What is your hypothesis: will heart rate increase, decrease, or stay the same after consuming caffeine? Why?
✍️ Prior Knowledge & Hypothesis
2
Measure the resting heart rate (baseline). Sit quietly for 5 minutes, then count your pulse for 30 seconds and multiply by 2. What is your baseline BPM? Why is it important to rest before measuring?
✍️ Baseline Measurement & Reasoning
3
Consume one standard serving of the caffeinated beverage. Record the type and estimated caffeine content. At 15, 30, and 60 minutes after consumption, measure your heart rate again. What pattern do you observe in the data?
✍️ Observations & Patterns
4
If you were designing this experiment more rigorously, what variables would you need to control? What are the limitations of this simple classroom version of the experiment?
✍️ Variables & Limitations
