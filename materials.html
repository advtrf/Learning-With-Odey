What are Additive Substances?

Food additives are natural or synthetic substances intentionally added to food or beverages during processing, packaging, or storage. Their purpose is to improve taste, appearance, texture, nutritional value, or shelf life — not to be consumed as food on their own. In Indonesia, the use of food additives is regulated by BPOM (Food and Drug Supervisory Agency).

"So basically.. additives are why your instant noodles taste AMAZING but your homemade soup tastes is delicious!🍜"
🍭

Colorants (Food Coloring)

Improve visual appeal of food & beverages

Colorants are substances added to restore color lost during processing or to make food more visually appealing. They are divided into natural colorants (derived from plants, animals, or minerals) and synthetic colorants (chemically manufactured). While both are permitted with proper dosage, some synthetic dyes such as Rhodamine B and Metanil Yellow are strictly banned because they are toxic and carcinogenic.

Sweeteners

Provide sweet taste, some are low or zero calorie

Sweeteners are food additives that give a sweet flavor to food and beverages. They can be classified as nutritive sweeteners (provide calories, e.g. sucrose, fructose) or non-nutritive / artificial sweeteners (extremely sweet with little to no calories, e.g. saccharin, aspartame). Artificial sweeteners are widely used in "diet" or "sugar free" products and are generally safe within the Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI) set by WHO.

Preservatives

Inhibit microbial growth to extend shelf life

Preservatives prevent or slow the growth of bacteria, mold, and yeast that cause food spoilage. They extend the shelf life of products without necessarily changing taste or appearance. Like all additives, their use is strictly regulated. Some preservatives such as formalin (formaldehyde) and borax are strictly prohibited in food as they are hazardous to human health.

Flavorings (Flavor Enhancers)

Enhance or intensify the savory taste of food

Flavor enhancers do not have a strong taste on their own, but they amplify the existing flavors — especially the umami (savory) taste — in food. The most well-known is Monosodium Glutamate (MSG), derived from glutamic acid. When used within recommended limits, MSG is considered safe by FDA and BPOM. Natural flavorings include ingredients like salt, sugar, vinegar, and various spices.

Aroma Enhancers (Artificial Flavorings)

Give food a characteristic scent and flavor profile

Artificial flavorings are chemical compounds that mimic the aroma and taste of natural ingredients. They are widely used in processed foods when using actual natural ingredients would be too costly or impractical. Common examples include vanillin (artificial vanilla), ethyl butyrate (artificial pineapple), and isoamyl acetate (artificial banana).

Thickeners & Stabilizers

Improve texture, consistency, and stability of food

Thickeners and stabilizers give food products a desirable texture — smooth, creamy, or gel-like. They prevent ingredients from separating (e.g., oil and water in salad dressing) and improve the mouthfeel of products. Most come from natural sources like seaweed (agar, carrageenan) or plant starches.

What are Addictive Substances?

Addictive substances are chemical compounds that affect the central nervous system, altering mood, perception, or consciousness. Regular use leads to dependence — the body craves the substance to function normally. In Indonesia, they are regulated under Law No. 35/2009 (Narcotics) and Law No. 5/1997 (Psychotropics). Misuse can cause severe physical and psychological damage.

Narcotics

Substances that reduce pain and cause strong dependence

Narcotics are substances derived from plants or synthesized chemically that cause reduced or lost consciousness and relieve pain. Under Indonesian law, narcotics are classified into three schedules based on their medical use potential and addiction risk. Misuse leads to physical dependence, withdrawal symptoms, and potentially death by overdose.

Schedule Characteristics Examples
Schedule I Very high addiction risk; NO medical use; prohibited entirely Heroin (putaw), cocaine, marijuana (high THC)
Schedule II High addiction risk; limited medical use (last resort only) Morphine, pethidine, fentanyl
Schedule III Moderate addiction risk; used in therapy under strict supervision Codeine (cough medicine), buprenorphine

Psychotropic Substances

Affect mental state, emotion, and behavior

Psychotropics act on the brain and nervous system to alter perception, emotion, and behavior. Unlike narcotics, they do not necessarily relieve physical pain. They include stimulants (speed up CNS), depressants (slow down CNS), and hallucinogens (distort perception). They are also classified in schedules (I–IV) under Indonesian law.

Other Addictive Substances

Legal but harmful substances found in daily life

Not all addictive substances are illegal. Many are encountered in everyday life. Despite being legally available, they carry significant health risks and can cause dependence. These include nicotine (tobacco), alcohol (ethanol), and caffeine. These substances are not classified as narcotics or psychotropics but are regulated due to their addictive nature.
